What Is the Furthest I Have Ever Been from Home?

The furthest I have ever been from home is when I first moved to Mexico. Although the distance itself was considerable, what made the experience so eye-opening is the fact that it not only showed me the size of the world, but also the fact that the world is comprised of so many different and diverse cultures that fill this rock we call Earth. The fact that I was so far away from home made me realize that the distance wasn’t just a physical thing, but also a realization of how I perceive the world around me.

As soon as I got there, it was as if I had moved to another planet. Everything was different – the language, the food, the speed of life, especially since I couldn’t understand a word of what was being said. I mean, what’s the difference between me talking to someone who doesn’t understand Spanish and an alien who doesn’t understand Spanish either? As time passed by, I began to understand that maybe it wasn’t them, but me. It was my lack of communication that made everything seem so foreign and distant.

However, the longer I was away, the less uncomfortable I was and the more I came to appreciate the culture. Being in Mexico made me realize that I needed to pay attention and be more open to new experiences, which in turn allowed me to question some of the cultural norms back home that I realized were completely unnecessary. The more I thought about it, the more I realized that my discomfort was a result of my own expectations and not a problem with the culture itself.

Being so far away also gave me a new perspective on home. It made me realize how much I valued community and family. Our shared experiences, cultural norms, and sense of shared identity are all incredibly important to us as human beings. The feeling of belonging to something, things that I had previously taken for granted, suddenly became much more important to me once they were taken away. It is in this way that I began to realize that perhaps it wasn’t home that had changed, but my perspective on it.

In the end, my time in Mexico was the most removed from what I consider to be home that I have ever experienced, both physically and mentally. It was through this distance that I was forced to realize that perhaps it wasn’t the world that was foreign to me, but rather myself being outside of what I knew. It was through this removal from what I knew that I was able to get a bird’s-eye view of home and a better understanding of what it means to me.
